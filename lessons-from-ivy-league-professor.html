Lessons from having dinner at an IVY League Professor’s House

Sep 30 2022 3 min read

I will keep the names of people and the college anonymous.

A close friend of mine and I had spent our Junior to Senior year of High School summer working on our respective projects; although they were separate, we worked together.

Traveling 20 stops on the train to Central park to move closer to our end goals.

Fast-forward to the beginning of Senior Year, I had published my book, and he had been semi-established in his field. He was fortunate enough to have an established figure pick up his immaculate Op-Ed and present it in front of a grand audience.

Top organizations were flying him out to do guest speeches, spectate different events, provide perspective, and so on.

While I was suffering at football practice, he flew around, met famous people, and became pretty well known.


One day he told me that a school (which we both had at the top of our college list) reached out to him and would like to invite him over to speak with him and share perspectives.

To my delight, he invites me as a guest.

Two confident young teens of color, identifying strongly with their intellectual side, get on a train and head to the college.

Along the way, we do what any young men on somewhat of an upward curve do. We come up with bodacious & elaborate plans & predictions for the future.

When they talk to us, they have to admit us.

Who else is doing this, bro?

We are one of a kind. (Fastforward, and we are one of the kinds that get rejected from the school. I’ll save that for a future piece)


We get there.

We talk with the faculty, the pretentious students, and the down-to-earth students that chill in the corner.

They were impressed, but that’s beside the point.

We noticed that everyone was quite strange, but not strange to each other.

They were different, but not different from one another.

They were in a bubble. (I’ll speak more on that in another piece)

We talk about ourselves and ideas, and they speak about their little academic drama and how Classics MUST teach Latin. And how not teaching Latin is a DISSERVICE to all of humanity. How will they be able to learn?


Fast forward a few hours, and we are having dinner at a professor’s house.

Their hair is down (figuratively), and we hear some pretty outlandish things.

With a mix of an experience that convinced us that we were practically admitted and going behind the curtain, we were almost dissuaded from wanting to go.


You realize it’s all a facade.

These professors aren’t geniuses; they’re just smart & just dedicated their lives to a field. (Over exaggeration for some of them are geniuses, but it’s to say they’re not special)

These students are brilliant but not all good. They live in a bubble that is almost impossible to escape from. They are building their intelligence seemingly infinitely but have no intentions of ever doing anything with it.

During our time at dinner in the professor’s house, we thought:

Is it this easy?
This can easily be me.
Is everyone, even the most intelligent and most established figures, like this behind closed doors?

It was indeed an experience.


There are many different worlds out there.

There are many we have experienced, many we have yet to experience, and many we will never experience.

Getting a glimpse into this world changed my outlook on my world.

I had held this world to the highest standard. It was mystical, mysterious, and magnificent. It was greater than anything else to me.

So you can imagine how seismically perspective shifting it would be, for it just to be another world.

(I’m lowkey lying. I’m still doing everything I can to get admitted into that world. I will speak more on this seeming disappointment by-> still wanting to be accepted by phenomena in another piece.)

Cheers!

I hope you are all doing well.

Ethan Castro

Some random dude who is somehow having an effective gap year.
