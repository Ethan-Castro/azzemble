Finding vs Creating Myself

Oct 2 2022 3 min read

“Yea, I am taking a gap year to find myself. I don’t know what I want to do, so I will travel, have fun, and meet new people.”

I am taking a gap year to create myself.

“But Ethan, fate is already written. You just go with the course of your life.”

Who’s to say (1) I don’t write my future. (2) This is my fate. To create the person I desire to be.

I don’t want to study all day. I don’t want to code.

I’d much rather sit on a velvet loveseat surrounded by people that I have convinced that I am this intelligent dude and entrance them with every word I say while I try my best to act like these words are just rolling off of my tongue and this isn’t the product of thousands of hours of talks with my dad or random lectures I’ve watched at night.

But I have a burning desire to become a person with “a particular set of skills, skills that I will acquire over a very long gap year”.



On the one hand, I can say

“I love doing . I feel the fulfillment of doing it at my core. It’s what I am meant to do.”

Is that actual fulfillment? Or is it pleasure? Is my purpose? Is it the reason I am here, now, in this vessel or is it something that gives me a mere sense of identity?

On the other hand, I can choose which side I want.

() Love what I do / () Love who I am

I can live a fulfilled life if I love who I am and who I’ve become.
If I simply love what I do, it can act as a distraction. Loving what I do for the wrong reason can simply be loving the feeling I get from doing whatever I am doing (which can be many negative things).

I am a month into this gap year. A month into teaching myself new things, things I once despised — a month into pivoting my passions (without exterminating my old ones).

I am a month in, and I’ve made a year of progress.

Where is the limit? Can I really teach myself to love something? Can I truly familiarize myself with foreign concepts in a few weeks? What can I do in a year or more?

Don’t get it twisted.

I did not hate my ‘old self’, but I wasn’t who I wanted to be.

I knew that when I left my school environment, I needed a new reason to walk around with enormous confidence and a new way to acquire some sort of respect, which [I think] I had. Sports wouldn’t cut it anymore. Being charismatic with not much to back up for it wouldn’t cut it anymore. Only being 17 years old wouldn’t cut it anymore. No one has to help me anymore. Staying stagnant is unsustainable.

If you’ve talked to me, I sound the same and talk pretty similar; it’s just a matter of my intentions, goals, and work ethic.

I admired many figures I saw online to the point which I had to reflect and question why and to what extent.

I then did some background research, and the admiration dwindled into an appreciation.

Then, I realized I can do things just as great as them, and the appreciation turned into a respect for them to have [seemingly] disciplined themself and fulfilled their potential.

I am not going to go on a huge trek.

Solve the riddles to get the next clue and go to the next location.

Reach the final destination. Dig up a box. Dust it off. Put the code in and discover myself.

I will try everything in my power to consciously self-reflect, logically plan, receive perspective from trusted advisors (parents, friends, experienced people), and plan out who I want to become.

The process of finding will just be finding out how.

This is just my philosophy on the matter. I will not debate whether we have free will or if our fate is already written.

I understand if you passionately disagree with everything I’ve said or find it illogical. This is a matter in that you usually take one side very strongly.

(I did not speak on faith, but it is a very major factor)

Ethan Castro

Some random dude who is somehow having an effective gap year.
