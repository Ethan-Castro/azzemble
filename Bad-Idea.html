Was the Gap Year a Bad Idea? | 1 Month

Sep 24 2022 3 min read

Was it a good idea to venture into an entirely new field, with no oversight/guidance/prior knowledge/backup/clear plan, while delaying your life a year?

The only friend I see is my good friend Hazem, once every other week, and maybe two others that commute to college from home once a month.

You may be thinking:

Damn, this dude is a loser.

or, Damn, this dude seems lonely.

I can confidently say I do not feel lonely anymore… I cannot speak on my loser status at this current time.

Let me walk you through my usual schedule:

  • 5 am | Wakeup
  • 5:30 am | Go to Starbucks when they open
  • 5:30–40 am | Chat with the Baristas who have become my friends
  • 5:40 am–12:30 pm | Deep Work Session: Programming Project or Learning
  • 12:30 pm -2:30 pm | Gym
  • 2:30–6 | Finish what I was working on earlier or Start a new course/project for the next morning’s deep work session
  • 6–8 | Jiu Jitsu or continue previous
  • 8–10:30 | Chill with fam
  • 10:30 | Sleep, then restart

It’s rare for my schedule to deviate from that.

Yes, it’s objectively monotonous, boring, and lacking of anything fun, but it does not feel that way.

I’ve grown to love it.

What choice do I have other than to love it?

To love seeing foreign concepts become native within the same day.

To love the feeling of annoyance and anger that nothing is working ->, realizing the worse I feel at the moment of confusion, the more satisfactory grasping it will feel later ->, experiencing that joyous feeling of understanding -> learning to love that mental resistance of immersing myself in the unknown.

To love writing on my silly medium account and getting little hearts on my Instagram story.

To love seeing progress in the mirror.

To love submitting the same guy that made me almost pass out a few weeks ago.

To love that my friends behind the Starbucks counter make my drink without me scanning or even saying what I want.

I realized the only way not to hate it is to love it.

Imagine Sisyphus happy.

Luckily, it doesn’t roll back down once I push the boulder up. It’s continuous progress; all I have to do is push.

All I have to do is focus, work, learn, and somehow I feel more intelligent and more effective; my chances of getting into an amazing college increase, and my future career earnings/options grow exponentially.

The progress is so apparent (to me) that it’s almost as if I am watching myself from an outsider’s perspective.

It’s exhilarating to see this kid go from thinking he was going to get into an Ivy League College -> to barely getting into any colleges -> to having no clear path -> choosing to take a gap year -> to now.

It feels like a long time, but if I think about it logically for half a second, I realize this is all happening at the speed of light.

I will not share everything. I will save it and spread it across a few pieces.

In objective + universal metrics, I am a year behind (pushing back college), but I am light years ahead of where I thought I would’ve at 20.

Was this a risky decision? Yes.

Is this still risky? Yes.

Don’t worry. I am still very conscious of the situation.

Thank you to everyone who likes the stories of my articles and “Have a great day” stories.

Thank you to those who message me about my pieces.

Thank you to those who like my coding progress updates.

Thank you to those who view my story.

Thank you to those who send me resources they think would help me.

Thank you to those who allow me to be on my journey without constantly berating me with questions intended to make me feel lesser than for taking this route.

Thank you to everyone who doesn’t know what I am doing (or maybe disagrees with it) but still encourages me.

Thank you to everyone.

Ethan Castro

Some random dude who is somehow having an effective gap year.
